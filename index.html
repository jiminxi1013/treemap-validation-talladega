Introduction

About Me

I am a PhD student and Research Assistant at the Institute of Artificial Intelligence, University of Georgia. My work sits at the intersection of remote sensing, spatial statistics, and forest ecology.

My current research focuses on the rigorous validation of large-scale forest mapping products — critically evaluating whether imputation-based approaches achieve genuine spatial predictive skill beyond population-level summaries.

I develop novel evaluation frameworks that go beyond conventional metrics, applying concordance correlation decomposition, scale-dependent accuracy analysis, and variance retention statistics to expose limitations invisible to standard RMSE and bias reporting.

Primary Project

Validation of Imputation-Based Forest Mapping Products

I investigate the accuracy limits of wall-to-wall forest attribute products generated via nearest-neighbor imputation from LiDAR-derived metrics. A central question driving my work: do these products achieve genuine spatial predictive skill, or do they merely reproduce population-level means? My field validation in Talladega National Forest, Alabama, using 255 independent plots and 15,870 measured trees, reveals a systematic mean-structure decoupling paradox — algorithm updates that reduce bias can simultaneously destroy rank correlation, a failure mode invisible to conventional evaluation metrics.

Conference Poster · 2024 · PERSEUS Project

Validating TreeMap Vegetation Data Using Field Measurements from Talladega National Forest

Jiayi Tu, Frederick W. Maier, Khaled Rasheed  ·  Institute for Artificial Intelligence, University of Georgia
Abstract
This poster presents a validation of TreeMap 2016 predictions — diameter, height, basal area, and volume — against 2022 field measurements from 255 FIA plots in Talladega National Forest. Tree locations were spatially aligned using azimuth-distance mapping and convex hull boundaries, with area-weighted aggregation across overlapping pixels.

Results show weak correlations across all attributes (R² = 0.005 for diameter, 0.028 for height, 0.111 for basal area, 0.081 for volume), with systematic underestimation particularly in the upper quantiles. CDF comparisons confirm a consistent leftward shift in TreeMap predictions relative to field observations, indicating limited reliability for fine-scale stand-level analysis.

Supported by USDA NIFA #2023-68012-38992 (PERSEUS — Promoting Economic Resilience and Sustainability of the Eastern U.S. Forests), with assistance from the UGA Warnell School of Forestry and Natural Resources.

Manuscript · 2024

Validation of USDA TreeMap for Southeastern Mixed Pine-Hardwood Forests: A Comprehensive Assessment of TreeMap 2016 and 2022

Jiayi Tu  ·  Institute of Artificial Intelligence, University of Georgia
Abstract
TreeMap provides 30-meter resolution forest attribute predictions across the conterminous United States, but rigorous independent validation in southeastern mixed forests remains limited. This study evaluated TreeMap 2016 and 2022 using field data from 255 plots (15,870 trees) in Talladega National Forest, Alabama, with area-weighted spatial overlay to address scale mismatches between 30 m pixels and 0.04 ha field plots.

Three findings emerge. First, scale-dependent analysis reveals a fundamental divergence: volume RMSE declined 84% (2016) and 83% (2022) from plot-level to 30-plot aggregations, confirming utility for regional biomass totals, while R² degraded monotonically across the same scales, confirming absence of spatial rank-ordering skill. Second, a mean-structure decoupling paradox is documented: TreeMap 2022 reduced tree-level bias while volume CCC collapsed from 0.416 to 0.164 and variance retention fell from 79% to 40%. Third, CCC decomposition (ρ/C_b separation) consistently revealed degradation invisible to conventional metrics.

TreeMap is appropriate for regional biomass accounting and landscape-scale inventory summaries. It is unsuitable for spatial mapping of structural variation at any scale. Evaluation frameworks should report CCC with ρ/C_b decomposition, variance retention, and scale-dependent R² alongside RMSE.
